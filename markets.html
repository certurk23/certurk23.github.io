US Equity Indices Explained

The S&P 500 is the primary benchmark for US large-cap equities — a market-cap-weighted index of 500 companies across all sectors. The NASDAQ 100 is technology-heavy, giving it a higher growth and volatility profile. The Dow Jones Industrial Average tracks 30 blue-chip stocks and is price-weighted — making it an imperfect but widely reported market gauge. The Russell 2000 measures small-cap performance and tends to lead in risk-on environments. Divergence between NASDAQ and Russell 2000 — tech leading while small-caps lag — often signals a narrow, momentum-driven rally rather than broad market health.

Forex & Currency Markets

The foreign exchange market is the world's largest and most liquid financial market, with over $7.5 trillion in daily trading volume. EUR/USD — the world's most-traded currency pair — reflects the relative monetary policy divergence between the ECB and the Federal Reserve. GBP/USD is sensitive to UK economic data, Bank of England decisions, and post-Brexit trade dynamics. USD/JPY has become a critical macro risk signal: when the Bank of Japan maintains ultra-loose policy while the Fed tightens, the yen weakens and carry trades build. Sharp yen reversals (JPY strengthening) are associated with rapid global carry trade unwinding and equity market stress.

Macro Indicators & Yield Curve

The macro panel tracks Fed Funds Rate expectations, 10Y/2Y Treasury yield spread (yield curve inversion indicator), and cross-asset correlation signals. Yield curve inversion — when short-term rates exceed long-term rates — has historically preceded US recessions by 12–18 months and remains a key input in quantitative macro allocation models. The VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) measures the 30-day implied volatility of S&P 500 options and serves as a real-time fear gauge: VIX below 15 signals complacency, 20–30 indicates elevated concern, and spikes above 40 historically coincide with forced institutional deleveraging and systemic stress events.

Gold, Commodities & Crypto

Gold is the premier macro hedge asset, exhibiting negative correlation to real interest rates: when real yields fall (nominal rates minus CPI inflation), gold tends to appreciate as the opportunity cost of holding a non-yielding asset decreases. WTI crude oil responds primarily to OPEC+ production decisions, US inventory data from the EIA, and global demand forecasts. Bitcoin has evolved into an institutional asset class tracked alongside gold in macro allocation models — sensitive to liquidity conditions, dollar strength, and risk appetite cycles. During periods of dollar weakness and loose monetary conditions, both gold and Bitcoin tend to outperform traditional fixed income.
